RoboBOAT - An Autonomous Boat to carryout Coral and Bathymetric Mapping

Introduction

During the Summer of 2022, I got the opportunity to be part of the Mid-Atlantic Robotics IN Education (MARINE) program of the Bermuda Institute of Ocean Sciences (BIOS). Mentored by Dr. Jnaneshwar Das, laboratory director of Distributed Robotic Exploration and Mapping Systems (DREAMS) Laboratory, Arizona State University., and Kaitlin Noyes, Director of education and community engagement at Bermuda Institute of Ocean Sciences (BIOS) for making this happen.

I, along with Rodney Staggers Jr had taken our lab’s two prototype autonomous vehicles for mapping and monitoring coral reef communities in Bermuda. These two vehicles, a robotic boat (the “RoboBoat”) and an underwater drone (the “UDrone”) were among the technologies highlighted in the “Innovations for the Environment” workshop during the first week of my stay and test their capabilities in the open ocean in the following week. During the workshop, I interacted with Bermudian High school students and shared my knowledge of Autonomous Vehicles and Simulation environments in the OpenUAV platform. During the workshop and the following week, we deployed the RoboBoat in the open ocean and carried out autonomous missions, testing its endurance. We also mounted a GoPro camera and a Ping 360 sonar to gather data on the coral reefs. Upon processing the data and using the concept of Structure From Motion, we recreated the 3D model of a certain portion of the coral reef. The field testing of these vehicles made me realize that testing in laboratory environments is totally different from testing in actual fields. I learned how to make quick fixes and troubleshoot problems without the Internet since there was no reception in the ocean.

Novelty

Both the vehicles were designed in such a way that they can be transported easily and can be set up and deployed in a short period of time. The electronics can be interchanged between them which makes it a versatile and modular design. I also had the chance to meet Sean Kelley, AUV Sentry Program Manager at Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution, and learned a lot from him about how large-scale autonomous vehicles are deployed in the ocean. It helped to compare and contrast the autonomous vehicles we develop and the ones they make at WHOI.

Conclusion

Overall, it was a memorable experience, and I was able to learn a lot about the field deployment of autonomous vehicles and how automation can help the Oceanography Research Community and their needs.
